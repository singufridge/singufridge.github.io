Reading Response 1 - Garden of Forking Paths

My key takeaways from the reading were:

  • I thought the content was very interesting and offered quite an artistic and abstract insight into the kind of content we're covering in class, specifically as it relates to version control and "travelling through time." The concept introduced in the story of infinite realities and versions of yourself being created when you are faced with a decision relates back to what you can do with version control. This is a tool that lets you look forward and back in time in a similar manner, peering into different states of development and choosing to branch off into another timeline or to look back into the future.
  • I also think Borges is trying to convey something interesting about destiny and free will, though the concept of infinite realities seems to conflict with an inescapable destiny, the events of the ending still feel set in stone somehow, even though the main character could've made different choices. I think this is evident in the way that the futures are described as more of a tapestry than something being decided in time.
  • One idea that struck me was that the messaging seemed to be warning against the sheer overwhelm of the concept of infinite realities, and that to focus too much on their infinitie nature is to miss the what is happening in the present.

Reading Response 2 - Glitch Studies Manifesto

My key takeaways from the reading, in question format:

  1. How does this connect to the techniques or concepts we are working with in code?

    This manifesto very much focuses on the general design philosophy and thematic relevance of "the glitch." In utilizing concepts of the glitch, such as deconstruction, repetition, and disruption, the glitch can be integrated into any artistic medium in order to convey these themes. This design philosophy is especially relevant for web design as it is a digital medium, and, as Menkman writes, a glitch breaks our expectations for how a digital medium is meant to function.

  2. What does this challenge or complicate for you?

    The glitch as a design philosophy challenges our expectations of how technology is supposed to serve us and display information. In the presence of a glitch, technology suddenly becomes more incomprehensible, more distant, more interrupted, though it is all a matter of human perception nonetheless. For me, this complicates what I had previously thought of glitches as being concrete, always symptomatic of an objective problem. However, the reading made me consider ways in which the glitch could be used to simulate a feeling, just like any other artistic technique.

  3. What surprised you?

    Like I mentioned, the reading surprised me in the way that it defined glitches as being based on perception, but what also surprised me were the ways in which the glitch was likened to absurdism/surrealism. Now that I think about it, the comparison makes a lot of sense--the glitch as a way to convey distance and "nonsense" in the way a surrealist painter would on a canvas.

  4. What are your key takeaways?

    • The glitch is a design philosophy that reflects themes of deconstruction, repetition, and disruption
    • The glitch can be technological and/or based on human perception
    • The glitch is similar in contruction to a kind of technological absurdism
  5. Where did this take your imagination?

    I was thinking throughout the whole manifesto what kinds of ways I could integrate this interesting philosophy into my own website design. I saw the way the pages were constructed, with odd spacing in paragraphs and letters, empty characters, visual noise, and other glitchy qualities that got my mind thinking. Some of the ideas I got while reading were:

    • a button that keeps jumping just below your mouse when you hover over it so you can't click it
    • a pixelated noise map overlaid over an image with a cool blending mode applied to it
    • visible text labelling every html element (eg. "<div>" visible in every corner of every div)


Reading Response 3 - Net Art Gone Wrong

My key takeaways from the reading:

  • In this week's reading, failiure becomes a mode of artistic expression when reframed into the net art medium. However, failiure is also percevied differently by different users. Especially when 'failiure' is defined within a digital space, as 'glitches' can look different depending on how tech savvy you are. For example, Jodi.org was perceived as a failiure in the eyes of many who did not delve deeper to further understand it purposeful aesthetic of failiure.
  • As White says, repetition in other forms of art is received quite differently than it is with net art, as the way this digital medium is costructed almost relies on repetition as a tool for expression.
  • Admittedly, I didn't really understand the author's comparison of the aesthetic of failiure to the feminist movement. I understand they both share the breaking down of social norms, but the usage of the language "ideological failiure" did not sit right with me. Why not draw the comparison of the punk movement instead, where social norms are all but encouraged to be dissected and broken down? I was also confused by the reasoning that those who are more connected to technology are "feminized" in a way, and I felt the gender essentialist assumptions being made were mostly speculative.

Reading Response 4 - {Software} Structures

My key takeaways from the reading, in question format:

  1. How does this connect to the techniques or concepts we are working with in code?

    I think this article is a really interesting example of the interpretive artistic meeting the inert technical and what kind of processes/creations are made when the two collide. I thought the processes of the "wall drawings" were really interesting because that is the kind of thought process I start with whenever I want to make something visual with code.

  2. What does this challenge or complicate for you?

    Not a lot, I just found the reading interesting as an exploration and I think it's a good mindset of "translation" to take into this upcoming assignment.

  3. What surprised you?

    Honestly, the concept of the "wall drawings" and then the "translation" of those ideas being presented as code was really cool and stood out to me. I usually have a hard time translating other people's ideas into the image that they have in their head, so I tried to read each wall drawing and picture it in my head, and almost every time I was surprised by what the translation looked like.

  4. What are your key takeaways?

    Translating art into code can be a complicated task and is very human and fallible. Though we are always coming up with structures to help us, they also limit us, and this is sometimes frustrating and sometimes inspiring.
